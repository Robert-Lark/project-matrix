Goldmine Is a Type System

What forty years of arguing about VG+ can teach you about enums, honest schemas, and the limits of both.

July 17, 2026 · 7 min read

Last month I graded a copy of Bitches Brew — a 1970 stereo pressing, the one with the gatefold that never survives — and I sat with it under the desk lamp for a long time. There is a hairline on side three you can see if you tilt the disc against the light, and cannot hear at all. Is that VG+ or NM? The answer matters: on a record like this it is a forty-dollar question. And the longer I sat there, the more the question felt familiar, because deciding what a value is allowed to be is most of what I do for a living.

The Goldmine Grading Standard is the closest thing record collecting has to a spec.1 It admits a small, closed set of grades — Mint down through Poor — and every used record changing hands online is described in its vocabulary. It is, in other words, an enum. And like every enum I have ever shipped, it is a beautiful lie that mostly works.

Seven grades, forty years of argument

The standard's genius is its smallness. Mint. Near Mint. Very Good Plus. Very Good. Good Plus. Good. Fair. Poor. A seller in Osaka and a buyer in Ohio who share no language can still transact on the strength of three characters: VG+. That is what a good type does — it compresses a judgment into a token that travels.

But watch what the compression throws away. My Bitches Brew hairline is visual, not audible. Someone else's VG+ has surface noise between tracks but plays clean. A third copy has a pristine disc in a split sleeve. All three sell as VG+. The token is stable; the thing it names is a cloud.

Sleeve and media are graded separately for exactly this reason — one axis was never enough. Any schema that models condition as a single field has already lost the argument to a magazine from 1970s Iowa.

Engineers meet this constantly and keep being surprised by it. We define OrderStatus with five variants and then spend two years handling orders that are somehow between SHIPPED and DELIVERED. The enum was never wrong, exactly. It was a resolution decision — like choosing a film stock. Tri-X does not capture what Portra captures. You pick the grain you can live with.

There is also a quieter lesson in what the standard refuses to do: it has never versioned. No Goldmine 2.0, no breaking migration where VG+ splits into VG+A and VG+B. The maintainers understood, the way schema registries force you to understand, that a vocabulary shared by millions of strangers is effectively frozen the day it ships. You can annotate it, you can build conventions on top of it, but you cannot rename a variant that lives in a hundred thousand handwritten price stickers. Backward compatibility is not a policy. It is a property of having users.

An enum that lies politely

Here is roughly what the standard looks like when you make a compiler care about it. The interesting part is not the type — it is the parser underneath, where the real world leaks in:

// The eight grades the standard admits. Closed set. Allegedly.
type MediaGrade =
  | 'M' | 'NM' | 'VG+' | 'VG'
  | 'G+' | 'G' | 'F' | 'P';

// Everything sellers actually type, folded back into the spec.
function parseGrade(raw: string): MediaGrade {
  const g = raw.trim().toUpperCase().replace(/PLUS/g, '+');
  if (g === 'VG++' || g === 'NM-') return 'VG+'; // the folk grades
  if (isGrade(g)) return g;
  throw new GradeError(`unparseable grade: ${raw}`);
}

That second comment is the whole essay. VG++ is not in the standard. Neither is NM-, or "strong VG+", or "VG+ leaning NM, see photos." Sellers invented these because the enum's resolution was too coarse for the judgment they were trying to communicate, and they did it so consistently, across decades and continents, that any parser which rejects them is simply wrong about the domain it claims to model.

The half-grades people actually use

There is a name for this in the merchandising world: the folk taxonomy always beats the official one. When your users invent notation, they are filing a schema bug. You can close it as wontfix and normalize their input — which is what parseGrade does, quietly rounding a folk grade down to the nearest legal value — or you can widen the type and inherit forty years of ambiguity. Neither answer is free. The mistake is believing there is a third option where the world starts using your enum correctly.

Grading under the safelight

Photography taught me the part the spec leaves out. When you edit a contact sheet you do it under one lamp, at one brightness, with the same loupe every time — not because the light is objectively correct, but because judgment only converges when the conditions repeat. A print that looks muddy under the enlarger's safelight can sing in daylight. The judgment isn't in the eye; it's in the eye plus the room.

Serious sellers know this. Ask how a record was graded and the good ones will tell you the conditions: visually graded under 100-watt incandescent, or play-graded on a specific cartridge.2 The grade means nothing without the lamp. Which is to say: the enum value is only half the datum. The other half is provenance — who judged, under what conditions, against what reference. Most schemas store the token and discard the room.

The contact sheet is the same discipline applied to my own negatives. Thirty-six tiny frames on one sheet of paper, and the job is to grade them — this one prints, this one never will, this one maybe with work. The first pass is always too generous. Come back the next morning, same lamp, and half the circles you drew in grease pencil look like sentiment rather than judgment. Ratings drift; conditions anchor them. Every code review I have ever done works exactly like this, which is why I try never to approve anything after ten at night.

We shipped a version of this lesson at work, in the least glamorous corner you can imagine. A condition field went from a bare enum to an enum plus a free-text comment plus a photo array, and disputes dropped measurably — not because the enum got better, but because the schema stopped pretending the enum was the whole truth. The structured field settles the query plan; the unstructured fields settle the argument.

What I would tell the schema

So: keep the enum. Small closed sets are how strangers transact, and I have no patience for the view that typed vocabularies are oppression. But store the lamp. Alongside every judgment-shaped value in your schema, leave a place for the conditions of judgment — grader, method, date, dissent. It costs one nullable column now and saves you a redesign when the folk taxonomy shows up, because it always shows up.

The record went back in the sleeve as VG+, in the end, with a note about the hairline and a photo of it caught in the light. If the buyer disagrees with my grade, fine — but they will be disagreeing with my evidence, not my token. That is the most any type system can promise, and honestly, it is enough.
