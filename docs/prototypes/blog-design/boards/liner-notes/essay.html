Every Component Is a Reissue

What forty years of record pressings taught me about versioning a design system.

Written Vancouver, WA Revised twice Published July 17, 2026

I own two copies of the same record. Not by accident, and not because I forgot — the first is a 1973 pressing I bought in college for six dollars, and the second is a 2014 remaster I bought on purpose, at full price, knowing exactly what I was doing. They have the same catalog spine, the same songs in the same order, the same cover photo. And they are not the same record. The 1973 copy is warmer and a little blunted; the remaster is wider and brighter and, depending on the day, slightly exhausting. When someone at work asks me why version numbers are hard, this is the example I reach for.

Because here is the thing my day job and my record shelf keep telling each other: a version is not a sequence number, it’s a pressing. Same master, different plant, different year, different lacquer, different ears in the room. The music didn’t change. Everything around the music did. And whether the result counts as “the same record” is not a fact — it’s a judgment call the listener makes, usually with money.

Masters and releases

Discogs, where I work, is built on a distinction most software teams never make explicitly. A master is the abstract work: the recording, the thing you hum. A release is a specific physical (or digital) manifestation of it: this pressing, this plant, this year, this barcode. One master fans out into dozens of releases, and the database refuses to let you confuse them.1 Collectors argue about which release of a master to own the way engineers argue about which node version to pin.

MASTER — the abstract recording. RELEASE — one concrete manifestation: format + plant + year + region. The catalog treats the mapping as one-to-many, never one-to-one.

Design systems have the same shape and almost never admit it. There is the idea of your Button — its contract, its behavior, the promise it makes to product teams. And then there are its releases: v2.3.1 compiled against React 18, the fork someone vendored into the checkout flow in 2023, the “same” Button living in three bundles at three versions right now, in production, tonight. We talk as if there is one Button. The shelf says otherwise.

The split earns its keep the moment anything goes wrong. When a customer says a record sounds off, the first question is never “which album” — it’s “which pressing.” When a product team says the Button is broken, the first question should be the same, and in most orgs nobody can answer it. The bug report names the master; the bug lives in a release.

The canonical take

Collectors handle this ambiguity with a social protocol, not a technical one. For any master worth arguing about, the community converges on a canonical release — the pressing you mean when you just say the title. It isn’t always the first, and it isn’t always the best-sounding. It’s the one with the most trust accumulated around it. Design systems need the same thing and usually fake it badly: “latest” is not canonical, it’s just newest. Newest is a date. Canonical is a reputation.

The pressing plant is your build pipeline

A pressing plant takes a master it did not create and produces copies under local conditions: this vinyl formulation, this stamper wear, this quality control on this shift. That is a build pipeline. Your CI takes source it did not write and produces artifacts under local conditions — dependency resolution that day, compiler version, flags, the phase of the npm moon. Two builds of the same commit are the same master; they are not always the same release.

Once I started modeling it that way, the types wrote themselves:

// a release is a master plus pressing context
interface Master { id: MasterId; contract: PublicApi }
interface Release {
  master: MasterId
  pressed: Date       // build time, not merge time
  plant: Pipeline    // who stamped it, with what
  matrix: Diff        // what changed at the plant
}
const reissue = (m: Master, ctx: Plant): Release =>
  { master: m.id, pressed: ctx.now(), plant: ctx.id, matrix: diff(m, ctx) }

None of this is a new versioning scheme. It’s a new honesty about what a version is: not “the component, but later” — the component, plus everything the plant did to it. Semver tries to compress all of that into three integers, and the compression is lossy in exactly the way that makes 2 a.m. incidents.2

That inversion matters. Collectors don’t read a reissue’s hype sticker to learn what was remastered — they read it to figure out what was left alone. Original tapes? Original sequencing? Original cover? The sticker is a compatibility statement. The best changelog entries I’ve ever read work the same way: they spend as many words on the untouched surface as on the delta.

What collectors know that changelogs don’t

Spend an hour reading pressing-variant discussions and you’ll see a discipline most engineering orgs would envy. Nothing is “the good version.” Everything is this specific artifact, produced under these specific conditions, sounding this specific way — with evidence. The community catalogs variants it doesn’t even like, because the point of a catalog is coverage, not endorsement.

Compare the sleeves of two pressings of the same album sometime. Same layout, same type hierarchy, different ink, different year. Nobody at the label agonized over whether the reissue was “on brand.” The system absorbed the variation because the system was designed for variation:

That’s a design system doing what design systems are for. The grid holds; the ink varies. The identity lives in the constants, which means the variables are free.

The original pressing is not the truth. It’s just the first witness.

A friend who restores tape machines said that to me once about remasters, and I think about it constantly during API deprecations. v1 of your component is not sacred because it was right; it’s load-bearing because it was first, and everything after it gets judged as a departure. Knowing the difference between “correct” and “first” is most of the skill in maintaining a public interface.

The reissue checklist

So here is what shipping a new major of anything looks like at my desk now, translated straight off the record shelf:

  • Say what didn’t change, first. The compatibility statement is the headline; the delta is the fine print.
  • Name the plant. Build provenance in the artifact: toolchain, flags, lockfile hash. Two identical version strings from different pipelines are different releases.
  • Keep the old pressing in print. Deprecation windows are reissue programs. You don’t recall records; you let them coexist until the market moves.
  • Let canon be earned. Mark a version canonical only after real adoption — trust is accumulated, not declared in a tag.

None of these are radical. All of them fall out of one move: treating a version as an artifact with a context, instead of a number with an ego. The record industry figured this out because it had to — it spent a century shipping the same masters through changing plants, formats, and fashions, and its catalog survived all of it. Our components should be so lucky. The shelf is long, the music keeps playing, and every component is a reissue whether we press it deliberately or not.
