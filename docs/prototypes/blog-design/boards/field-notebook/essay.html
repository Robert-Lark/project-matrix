The Bin Has No Page Numbers

What twenty years of crate digging taught me about cursor pagination, and why offset/limit has been lying to you the whole time.

July 17, 2026

There is a record shop on Southeast Hawthorne where the jazz bin runs the full length of the back wall. I have spent more hours in front of it than I will admit to anyone who signs my performance reviews. A few Saturdays ago I was maybe two hundred records deep — far enough that my fingers had gone into that automatic flip-tilt-flip rhythm — when the owner walked over and filed a stack of fresh arrivals into the section I had already passed.

He did it politely. He even said sorry. But the damage was philosophical, not social: the bin I was digging through was no longer the bin I had started digging through. Everything behind my fingers had shifted. If I had been keeping count — I am four hundred and ten records in — that count was now wrong, and there was no way to know by how much.

I stood there with a water-damaged copy of Money Jungle in one hand and realized I was looking at the oldest bug in web engineering. The one we ship every time we paginate a list with OFFSET.

The lie of page 47

Offset pagination makes one promise: skip N rows, then give me the next twenty. It feels honest because it is easy to say in SQL and easy to render as numbered links at the bottom of a page. Page 47 of the search results. Page 12 of your collection. The numbers look like coordinates, like the list is a shelf with fixed positions.

But the promise only holds if the list holds still, and lists never hold still. Someone lists a record for sale while you browse. A seller deletes an item. A moderator merges two releases. Every mutation ahead of your offset silently reindexes everything behind it, and your next request skips a row you never saw or shows you one you already have. The classic symptoms — the duplicate item at the top of page 3, the listing your friend swears exists but you never encountered — are not rendering bugs. They are the bin shifting behind your fingers.

A concrete failure

Say you are on page 2 of a marketplace search sorted by newest, twenty items a page. Between your first and second request, three new listings arrive. The server dutifully skips twenty rows — but rows 21 through 23 are now the last three items of your old page 1, wearing new numbers. You see three duplicates and, worse, three listings you will never see at all, because they slid into a page you already turned. Nobody gets an error. Nobody gets a log line. The catalog just quietly lies to one person.

The failure mode of offset pagination is not a crash. It is a plausible-looking page of results that no consistent version of the data ever contained.

Leave a marker in the crate

What does the experienced digger do when they need to step away from the bin? They do not memorize I am four hundred and ten records in. They wedge a marker — a shop divider card, an old receipt, once in my case a parking ticket — against the record where they stopped. The marker is not a count. It is a position relative to an item, and it survives the owner filing new arrivals in front of it. That is a cursor.1

A cursor says: give me what comes after this record, where “this record” is identified by the thing you sorted on, not by how many rows happened to precede it that afternoon. In the Relay connection style, the client never even parses the marker — it is an opaque token the server mints and the client hands back:

// A cursor is a marker in the crate, not a count of records.
const { data, fetchMore } = useQuery(BIN_QUERY, {
  variables: { first: 20, after: null },
});

function onReachEnd() {
  const { endCursor, hasNextPage } = data.bin.pageInfo;
  if (!hasNextPage) return;
  fetchMore({ variables: { after: endCursor } });
}

The marker survives churn because it is anchored to an item, not an index. New arrivals filed ahead of it do not move it; deletions behind it do not move it. The worst case is that the anchoring record itself gets pulled from the bin — sold, merged, suppressed — and a well-built cursor degrades gracefully there too, because it encodes the sort key’s value, not the row’s identity alone: resume from “the first record after K-7789 in catalog order,” even if K-7789 is gone.

Our marketplace connections default to twenty items and cap at a hundred. The cap is not stinginess — every item on the page fans out to seller, pressing, and price-history lookups, and a hundred is where the dataloader math stops being funny.

What the digger already knew

Cursor pagination has a reputation for being fussy, and the fussiness is real: you need a stable sort. Here again the record bin got there first. Every serious shop files by a deterministic key — artist, then title, then, when the owner is the kind of person I want to be, catalog number as the tie-breaker. Not price. Not “vibe.” Not recently-touched. Because a sort that changes between visits makes every marker meaningless.

The engineering translation: sort by a unique, immutable compound key. created_at alone will betray you the first time two listings arrive in the same millisecond; created_at, id will not. If the user re-sorts the list, every old cursor is invalid by definition — that is not a bug, that is the owner re-filing the entire bin overnight, and any digger would expect their parking ticket to mean nothing the next morning.2

Looking forward and looking back

The part of the connection spec people skip is that cursors are bidirectional: after walks forward, before walks back. Diggers know this one too. The move where you pull a record, hold your spot with a thumb, and walk two records backward because you suddenly remember the sleeve you passed — that is last: 2, before: $thumb, and it only works because your thumb is in the crate, not in your memory of a number.

I no longer think of pagination as a UI problem or a database problem. It is a promise about what a list means while the world edits it, and the honest version of the promise has been worked out over decades by people flipping through bins of records — hold positions against items, sort by something that cannot change, and accept that when the shop re-files, your markers die. The database people wrote it down later. The diggers had it first.
